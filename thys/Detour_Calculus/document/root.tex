\title{The Detour Calculus}
\author{Manuel Eberl}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
This entry provides the \emph{detour calculus}, a relational framework to construct deformed versions of integration contours
by adding small indentations around problematic points (typically poles). It consists of:
\begin{itemize}
\item the definition of a relation that relates the original contour $\gamma$ to a family of deformed contours $(\gamma_\epsilon)_{\epsilon > 0}$
\item theorems that allow transferring geometric properties from $\gamma$ to $\gamma_\epsilon$ for sufficiently small $\epsilon$
\item a library of ``basic avoidance patterns'' to add indentations to lines, circular arcs, or the corners between them, and prove that they are in relation with the original contour
\item a calculus of inference rules which, in particular, allows dealing with composite contours by proving the relation for its individual parts separately
\end{itemize}
The motivating example for this calculus was the proof of the valence formula for modular forms (not included here), which requires such a complicated set of indentations that I deemed it beyond the reach of formalisation without a systematic approach such as the present one.
\end{abstract}
